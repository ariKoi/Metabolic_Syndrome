Data Description/Processing - Metabolic Syndrome In Rats

Published

April 29, 2025

1 Scope

The study is a comparative analysis of two different models of metabolic syndrome, high fat and high fat+high fructose, in rats using a metabolomic approach.

The main questions it attempts to answer are: Which of the two models is more suitable for drawing conclusions about the human pathology of metabolic syndrome? Is there a differentiation of the metabolomic profile between the two dietary models? Is either of the profiles closer to the average metabolic profile of an individual with metabolic syndrome? Does either of the two models reveal early biomarkers? If so, do any of them match known human markers? The experimental models were designed for a duration of 8 weeks, but the study was extended by an additional month.

This time extension led to an initial divergence of the metabolomic profiles, but gradual convergence thereafter. The question that arises is whether the final metabolic profile of metabolic syndrome is unique, but the pathways leading to it differ. What is the degree of success of each model in terms of the 5 key features of metabolic syndrome. Regarding the experimental design. The study included two different dietary models to induce metabolic syndrome in rats. The samples were analyzed at various time points (5) metabolomic analysis and biochemical markers. The data collected include hematological, biochemical and metabolomic measurements in blood.

Notes & Quick Descriptives

Data collection occurred in monthly intervals:

Time point 0: Baseline or pre-treatment

Time point 1: 1 month after nutrition plan treatment

Time point 2: 2 months after nutrition plan treatment

Time point 3: 3 months after nutrition plan treatment

Time point 4: 3 months after nutrition plan treatment (same as time point 3) but post-mortem measurements using a different blood sample extraction method than in the other time points.

The post-mortem clinical data i.e. weight, LDL, triglycerides etc. correspond to time point 4, and therefore match the metabolite data at time point 4. The match will be used to derive surrogate or substitute response variables (from the list of metabolites) for each variable of interest in the clinical data, since for the clinical data there is only 1 measurement in time. This is further described in the modeling page.

Time point 4 will be excluded from the longitudinal analysis since it was post-mortem.

At time point 0 there were differences in the age of rats, and those differences are currently not known. At time point 1, these age differences were corrected by sampling the rat measurements asynchronously. Hence and since time will be a covariate in all models, age is not considered as a covariate i.e. proportional to time since all rats have same age from time point 1. In addition, in order to correct for age differences at time point 0, the models will adjust for metabolites at time point 0.

Metabolite Data Descriptions & EDA

Metabolic syndrome is associated with several metabolites that reflect alterations in glucose, lipid, amino acid, and energy metabolism. Key metabolites include:

  1. Lipid Metabolites

Triglycerides (TG): Elevated in metabolic syndrome, indicating impaired fat metabolism. Free fatty acids (FFA): Increased levels contribute to insulin resistance and inflammation. Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) and Very-low-density lipoprotein (VLDL): Higher levels of these “bad” cholesterol types are common. High-density lipoprotein (HDL): Reduced “good” cholesterol levels are a marker of metabolic syndrome. Ceramides: Bioactive lipids linked to insulin resistance and cardiovascular disease.

  1. Glucose and Energy Metabolites

Glucose: Elevated fasting glucose is a diagnostic marker of metabolic syndrome. Insulin: High levels indicate insulin resistance. Lactate: Increased due to altered glycolysis and mitochondrial dysfunction. Ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate): Altered levels indicate disrupted fatty acid oxidation.

  1. Amino Acid Metabolites

Branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs) (Leucine, Isoleucine, Valine): Elevated levels are strongly linked to insulin resistance. Aromatic amino acids (Tyrosine, Phenylalanine): Increased levels are associated with obesity and diabetes risk. Glutamate and Glutamine: Higher glutamate and lower glutamine levels are associated with metabolic dysfunction.

  1. Inflammatory and Oxidative Stress Metabolites

C-reactive protein (CRP): A marker of systemic inflammation. Homocysteine: Elevated levels are linked to cardiovascular risk. Oxidized LDL: A marker of oxidative stress and atherosclerosis risk.

  1. Gut Microbiome-Derived Metabolites

Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) (Butyrate, Acetate, Propionate): Reduced levels may indicate an unhealthy gut microbiome. Trimethylamine-N-oxide (TMAO): Elevated levels are associated with cardiovascular disease risk. These metabolites collectively indicate metabolic dysfunction, insulin resistance, and increased cardiovascular risk. Monitoring and targeting these biomarkers through diet, exercise, and medications can help manage metabolic syndrome.

✅ Strongly Associated Biomarkers for Metabolic Syndrome:

  1. Trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) – Elevated levels are linked to cardiovascular disease, insulin resistance, and gut microbiota imbalance.
  2. Choline – Essential for lipid metabolism; imbalances are linked to NAFLD and insulin resistance.
  3. Serine – Low levels are associated with insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction.
  4. Glycine – Reduced in MetS; involved in antioxidant defense and glucose metabolism.
  5. Alanine – Increased in MetS; involved in gluconeogenesis and insulin resistance.
  6. Isoleucine – Branched-chain amino acid (BCAA) associated with obesity, insulin resistance, and metabolic dysfunction.
  7. Leucine – Elevated levels correlate with MetS, obesity, and T2D.
  8. Creatinine – A marker of muscle metabolism and kidney function, associated with insulin resistance and MetS progression.
  9. Taurine – Linked to lipid metabolism, blood pressure regulation, and oxidative stress.
  10. Betaine – Plays a role in homocysteine metabolism; imbalances can contribute to cardiovascular risk.
  11. Tyrosine – Elevated in insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction.
  12. Phenylalanine – Increased in MetS, linked to impaired glucose metabolism.
  13. Acetylcarnitine – Implicated in fatty acid metabolism dysfunction in MetS.
  14. Fructose – High intake contributes to insulin resistance and fatty liver disease.
  15. Glucose – A core biomarker of insulin resistance and diabetes.
  16. Mannose – Altered metabolism is linked to insulin resistance.
  17. Lactic Acid – Increased in mitochondrial dysfunction and metabolic disorders.
  18. Pyruvic Acid (Pyruvate) – Involved in glycolysis; alterations signal metabolic dysfunction.

🟡 Moderately Associated Biomarkers (Need Further Validation):

  1. Putrescine & Cadaverine – Polyamines linked to gut microbiota and inflammation in MetS.
  2. Sarcosine – Related to methylation and lipid metabolism, but its role in MetS is unclear.
  3. Histamine – Involved in inflammatory responses and metabolic homeostasis.
  4. Hypotaurine – Precursor to taurine; may help counter oxidative stress in MetS.
  5. Asparagine – Altered levels may reflect amino acid metabolism changes in MetS.
  6. Aspartic Acid – May be involved in energy metabolism and oxidative stress.
  7. Methionine – Essential for methylation reactions; altered levels may affect lipid metabolism.
  8. Lysine – Essential amino acid; its metabolic disturbances could relate to insulin resistance.
  9. Anthranilic Acid – A metabolite of tryptophan linked to inflammation and gut microbiota.
  10. Indole-3-Lactic Acid – Gut-derived metabolite with possible metabolic and inflammatory implications.
  11. 5-Hydroxy-L-Tryptophan (5OH-L-Tryptophan) – A serotonin precursor involved in neuroendocrine and metabolic regulation.
  12. Xanthurenic Acid – A tryptophan metabolite associated with insulin resistance and oxidative stress.
  13. Benzoic Acid – Related to gut microbiota metabolism, possibly linked to MetS.
  14. Xanthine & Hypoxanthine – Purine metabolism intermediates; increased levels may be linked to oxidative stress and insulin resistance.

❌ Less Relevant or Weak Evidence for MetS:

These metabolites are less directly linked to metabolic syndrome but may still have indirect roles:

  • Methylamine, Dimethylamine, Trimethylamine – Related to gut metabolism but not primary MetS markers. Monoisoamylamine – Limited data on its role in MetS.
  • Cytosine, Uracil, Thymine, Adenine, Guanine, Cytidine, Thymidine, Adenosine, Inosine, Uridine, Maltose, Ribose, Xylitol, Arabitol – These are nucleotides and sugars with limited direct associations to MetS.
  • Caffeine – May have protective effects against MetS but is not a biomarker.
  • Cotinine – A nicotine metabolite, not directly related to MetS.
  • Mannitol, Lactose, Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C) – Not considered primary biomarkers for MetS.

📌 Summary:

Strongest biomarkers: TMAO, Choline, Serine, Glycine, Alanine, BCAAs (Isoleucine, Leucine), Taurine, Betaine, Acetylcarnitine, Glucose, Lactic Acid, Fructose.

Moderate biomarkers: Polyamines (Putrescine, Cadaverine), Tryptophan metabolites, Histamine, Hypotaurine.

Less relevant metabolites: Nucleotides, sugar alcohols, caffeine.
